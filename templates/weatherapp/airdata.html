Definition: PM2.5 refers to particulate matter with a diameter of less than 2.5 micrometers (µm). These particles are tiny and can remain suspended in the air for long periods.

Simplification: While the technical definition of PM2.5 may not be entirely accurate, it serves as a simplistic way to explain the small size of these particles to the general public. Health Concerns: There has been a rapid increase in concern about the potential health impacts of PM10 (particulate matter with a diameter of less than 10 µm) in recent years. PM10 particles can cause respiratory issues and other health problems.

PM10 - "{{pm10}}"

Definition: PM10 refers to particulate matter with a diameter of less than 10 micrometers (µm). These particles are small, approximately 1/7th the thickness of a human hair.

Particle Size: PM10 particles are relatively large compared to PM2.5 and other fine particulate matter. Despite their larger size, PM10 particles can still remain suspended in the air and be inhaled into the respiratory system. Human Health Impact: PM10 particles can cause various health issues when inhaled, including respiratory problems such as asthma, bronchitis, and other lung diseases. They can also exacerbate existing cardiovascular conditions.

O3 - "{{o3}}"

Ozone Formation: Ozone (O3) is produced when nitrogen dioxide (NO2), hydrocarbons, and sunlight react. These pollutants are emitted from various sources.

Urban vs. Rural: Urban areas with high NO emissions have lower ozone levels due to NO's role in reducing ozone. Rural areas, with fewer NO emissions, experience higher ozone levels, particularly during hot, sunny weather ("summer smog").

NO2 - "{{no2}}"

Chemical Formula: NO2, which stands for nitrogen dioxide, is a chemical compound composed of one nitrogen atom (N) and two oxygen atoms (O). Its molecular formula is NO2.

Classification: NO2 belongs to the group of nitrogen oxides, which are compounds containing nitrogen and oxygen atoms.
Physical Properties:
Color: At higher temperatures, NO2 appears as a reddish-brown gas. Odor: NO2 has a characteristic sharp and pungent odor, which can be detected even at low concentrations.

SO2 - "{{so2}}"

Chemical Formula: SO2, which stands for sulfur dioxide, is a chemical compound composed of one sulfur atom (S) and two oxygen atoms (O). Its molecular formula is SO2.
Sources:
Natural Sources: SO2 can be released into the atmosphere through volcanic eruptions, forest fires, and other natural processes. Anthropogenic Sources: Human activities such as burning fossil fuels (coal, oil, and natural gas) for energy production, industrial processes (such as metal smelting), and transportation are significant sources of SO2 emissions.

CO - "{{co}}"

Chemical Formula: CO, which stands for carbon monoxide, is a chemical compound composed of one carbon atom (C) and one oxygen atom (O). Its molecular formula is CO.
Sources:

Incomplete Combustion: CO is produced when carbon-containing fuels such as gasoline, natural gas, coal, and wood are burned incompletely due to insufficient oxygen supply.
Vehicular Emissions: Automobiles and other vehicles are significant sources of CO emissions, particularly in urban areas with heavy traffic congestion.
